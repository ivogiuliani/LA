### Opportunità #9 — REDDIT_COMMENT · r/architecture · Score 15

**URL:** https://reddit.com/r/architecture/comments/1tc3l5v/why_is_concrete_not_common_for_building_houses/

**Titolo originale:** Why is concrete not common for building houses?

**Contesto:** Reddit r/architecture thread asks why concrete construction is common in Southeast Asia but rare for US residential homes.

**Draft:**

```
Architect working in LA here, so part of the answer is genuinely cultural. In most of southern Europe, Latin America and southeast Asia, residential construction defaulted to reinforced concrete and masonry for a long list of reasons: termites, humidity, earthquakes, fire, and the simple fact that skilled concrete labor is widely available and cheap. The US west and midwest defaulted to wood because timber was abundant, framing crews were everywhere, and code evolved around that supply chain.

What is shifting now is the math, specifically in California. WUI codes, Chapter 7A, IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home, and carrier underwriting are all converging to reward noncombustible structure. In high fire severity zones, the construction premium for concrete or ICF is increasingly offset by insurability and long-term loss avoidance. So the answer is less "concrete doesn't work in the US" and more "the US picked wood when nothing was pricing in fire, and that assumption is finally breaking in places like LA."

[My Villa — disclosure: we design reinforced-concrete homes in LA, so I'm not neutral, but the code/insurance shift is public record.]
```
