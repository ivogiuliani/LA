Insurance Will Decide How the Palisades Rebuild

Senator Allen's proposal signals a fundamental shift: the construction material is becoming the gatekeeper of rebuilding.

April 8, 2026 · 6 min read
Sources: Santa Monica Daily Press CDI / NAIC Study IBHS Sen. Ben Allen

On April 2nd, the Palisades Recovery Coalition and PaliBuilds hosted a forum with four candidates running for California Insurance Commissioner. The event surfaced a question that may define how — and whether — Los Angeles rebuilds in wildfire zones.

Senator Ben Allen proposed something that hasn't been part of the conversation before: requiring developers to demonstrate insurability before they are permitted to build in high-risk zones. Not fire compliance. Not code adherence. Insurability.

That distinction matters. A home can meet every building code requirement and still be uninsurable — or insurable only through the FAIR Plan at ruinous premiums. Allen's proposal flips the sequence: prove the market will insure you, then build.

The Data Behind the Shift

The numbers explain why this is happening now. A March 2026 study by the California Department of Insurance and NAIC found that rebuilding to the IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home standard could reduce projected wildfire losses by one-third.

But "wildfire prepared" is a spectrum. There's a meaningful difference between adding ember-resistant vents to a wood-frame house and building a structure that simply doesn't burn.

Non-Combustible vs. Fire-Resistant

Most fire-resistant retrofit solutions reduce risk at the margins: ember screens, tempered glass, Class A roofing. These are valuable — every layer of protection matters. But the structure itself remains combustible. Wood framing, which accounts for 94% of American single-family homes, is the fuel.

Reinforced concrete eliminates that variable entirely. It's not fire-resistant — it's non-combustible. There is no fuel to ignite. This is why concrete is the material of choice for structures that cannot be allowed to fail: museums, hospitals, data centers, critical infrastructure.

The question is no longer how to make wood homes safer in fire zones. The question is whether we should be using wood at all. IBHS, Wildfire Prepared Home Program

The insurance industry is already pricing this distinction. Mercury Insurance offers up to 50% discounts on wildfire premiums for homes built with non-combustible materials. The actuarial logic is simple: a structure that cannot burn poses a fundamentally different risk than one that resists burning somewhat better.

What This Means for the Rebuild

If Allen's proposal gains traction — and the political momentum suggests it will — the Palisades rebuild will face a new calculus. Developers and homeowners will need to answer: can this structure attract a carrier at a sustainable premium?

For wood-frame construction in a designated high-risk zone, that answer is increasingly uncertain. FAIR Plan enrollment surged 43% between September 2024 and December 2025. Major carriers continue to exit the California market. The gap between insurable and uninsurable is widening.

For non-combustible construction — concrete, steel, glass — the answer is structurally different. These materials don't just improve the odds; they change the category of risk. And that's what insurers care about.

Our Perspective

Senator Allen's proposal validates what we've believed from the start: construction material is not a finishing choice — it's a structural decision that determines whether a home is insurable, resilient, and built to last. Reinforced concrete doesn't just meet building codes. It meets the market's emerging definition of what a home in California should be.

The convergence is clear. Insurance pressure, legislative momentum, and actuarial data are all pointing in the same direction. The homes that will define the next chapter of Los Angeles construction won't just be beautiful — they'll be insurable by design.
